## Section 1 — Theory of operation: high-pressure oil injection system

The 6.0L Power Stroke uses a **HEUI** (Hydraulically actuated Electronically controlled Unit Injector) fuel system. Fuel injection is not commanded by fuel pressure alone — each injector is fired by **high-pressure engine oil** acting on a hydraulic plunger inside the injector. If the oil side cannot reach roughly **500 psi**, the injectors will not fire and the engine will not start, regardless of fuel pressure ([Bostech](https://bostechauto.com/blog/60-powerstroke-hpop-guide-what-you-need-to-know-before-replacing/), [Diesel Power Products](https://www.dieselpowerproducts.com/blog/6-0-powerstroke-no-start-no-problem/), [VehicleServicePros](https://www.vehicleservicepros.com/service-repair/underhood/article/21270176/diagnosing-a-no-start-on-a-diesel-60-liter-ford-powerstroke)).

**The oil path:**

1. **Oil pan → low-pressure oil pump (LPOP).** A camshaft-driven LPOP draws oil from the pan and routes it through the **oil cooler** and then the oil filter ([Bostech](https://bostechauto.com/blog/60-powerstroke-hpop-guide-what-you-need-to-know-before-replacing/)).
2. **Engine valley reservoir.** Filtered, cooled oil is delivered to a small high-pressure reservoir cast into the top of the engine block in the valley. This reservoir is the **HPOP intake** — the HPOP draws only from this reservoir, not directly from the pan ([Bostech](https://bostechauto.com/blog/60-powerstroke-hpop-guide-what-you-need-to-know-before-replacing/)).
3. **HPOP.** Mounted in the engine valley at the **rear of the engine, beneath the turbocharger**, the high-pressure oil pump is **driven by the camshaft gear**. 2003–early 2004 trucks use an aluminum swash-plate pump; 2004.5–2007 trucks use a cast-iron gear pump that is internally more durable but is "sabotaged" by the failing STC fitting ([Bostech](https://bostechauto.com/blog/60-powerstroke-hpop-guide-what-you-need-to-know-before-replacing/), [DrivingLine STC article](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/60l-power-stroke-problems-part-8-stc-fitting/)). Maximum demand pressure exceeds **3,000 psi** ([Bostech](https://bostechauto.com/blog/60-powerstroke-hpop-guide-what-you-need-to-know-before-replacing/)).
4. **STC fitting (2004.5–2007 only).** A snap-to-connect quick-disconnect joins the HPOP discharge to the **branch tube**. The two-piece coupler flexes under vibration and high pressure, and its internal seal degrades. Late failure leaks; complete failure blows apart ([DrivingLine](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/60l-power-stroke-problems-part-8-stc-fitting/), [Xtreme Diesel](https://www.xtremediesel.com/blog/2022/03/10/high-pressure-oil-pump-stc/)).
5. **Branch tube → standpipes → oil rails.** A Y-shaped branch tube splits oil to a standpipe on each cylinder bank. Standpipes thread into the cylinder heads and pipe oil into a long **oil rail** that runs the length of each head, under the valve cover. Each rail is sealed at its far end by a **dummy plug**. Both standpipes and dummy plugs use **O-rings** that are known to flatten and tear ([DieselHub standpipe guide](https://www.dieselhub.com/repair/6.0-power-stroke-standpipe-replacement.html), [Bostech](https://bostechauto.com/blog/60-powerstroke-hpop-guide-what-you-need-to-know-before-replacing/)).
6. **Oil rail → injector top.** Each rail meters high-pressure oil into the top of each injector. The injector's upper square O-ring seals this oil pathway. Lower O-rings seal the fuel side ([Riffraff Diesel](https://www.riffraffdiesel.com/blog/injector-orings-and-cups-failure-symptoms-and-causes-60l-and-73l-powerstroke/)).
7. **Injector firing.** Oil at >500 psi acts on the intensifier piston, pushing the fuel plunger and firing fuel into the cylinder. The **FICM** (Fuel Injection Control Module) sends a **48-volt, 20-amp** pulse to the injector solenoid that releases the high-pressure oil into the intensifier ([DrivingLine FICM article](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/60l-power-stroke-problems-part-7-ficm/), [Bullet Proof Diesel](https://bulletproofdiesel.com/blogs/diesel-tips-info/ford-6-0-ficm-symptoms-fuel-injection-control-module)).

**The two regulators — do not confuse them:**

- **IPR valve (Injection Pressure Regulator)** — physically threaded into the **HPOP cover plate at the rear of the engine, beneath the turbo up-pipe collector**. Its connector points up toward the hood. The PCM modulates its **duty cycle** (15 % fully open, 85 % fully closed). The IPR is the **mechanical actuator** that bleeds excess oil back to drain to control rail pressure. It contains an **internal screen** that can clog with debris ([DieselHub IPR](https://www.dieselhub.com/maintenance/6.0-powerstroke-ipr-valve.html), [Bostech IPR](https://bostechauto.com/blog/symptoms-of-failing-60-power-stroke-ipr-valve/)).
- **ICP sensor (Injection Control Pressure)** — a **three-wire variable-capacitance sensor** that reads actual oil-rail pressure and reports it to the PCM as a 0.2–5 V analog signal. Early (2003–early 2004) lives behind the turbo near the HPOP cover. Late (2004.5–2007) lives on the **passenger side valve cover** for easier access ([Bullet Proof Diesel ICP](https://bulletproofdiesel.com/blogs/diesel-tips-info/icp-sensor-6-0-powerstroke-function-symptoms-replacement), [DieselHub ICP](https://www.dieselhub.com/maintenance/6.0-powerstroke-icp-sensor.html)).

**What the PCM is doing during crank:** monitoring CKP for sync, commanding glow plugs through the GPCM, commanding the IPR closed (≈85 % duty) to build rail pressure, watching ICP rise, and once ICP > 500 psi and FICM SYNC = 1, releasing the FICM to fire injectors ([VehicleServicePros](https://www.vehicleservicepros.com/service-repair/underhood/article/21270176/diagnosing-a-no-start-on-a-diesel-60-liter-ford-powerstroke), [DieselHub Diagnostics Reference](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html)).

**Coolant-side cross-contamination matters.** The **oil cooler** sits between coolant and engine oil; when it plugs and ruptures, coolant enters the oil galleries → aerates the oil → the HPOP cavitates → ICP collapses. Coolant flow to the **EGR cooler** is downstream of the oil cooler, so when the oil cooler plugs, the EGR cooler overheats and ruptures, dumping coolant into the intake and combustion chamber ([Bostech EGR cooler](https://bostechauto.com/blog/understanding-the-60-powerstroke-egr-cooler-and-why-it-fails/), [Pure Diesel Power](https://puredieselpower.com/6-0-powerstroke-bad-egr-cooler-symptoms)).

## Section 3 — Canonical no-start diagnostic tests

### Test 1 — Full DTC pull (engine + trans modules)

- **Purpose:** Capture any stored faults across all modules before disturbing the vehicle. Specifically watch for ICP, IPR, FICM, CKP, CMP, glow plug, and injector circuit codes.
- **Prerequisites:** Functional OBD-II port; scan tool that supports Ford diesel modules (Ford IDS/FDRS, Snap-on, Autel MaxiSys, or ForScan + ELM327).
- **Procedure:**
  1. Key on, engine off (KOEO).
  2. Pull all stored and pending DTCs from PCM, FICM, TCM.
  3. Write down all codes verbatim — do **not** clear them until interpreted.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **P0611** + all eight injector circuit lows (P0261/0264/0267/0270/0273/0276/0279/0282) → classic failed FICM power supply ([Bullet Proof Diesel FICM](https://bulletproofdiesel.com/blogs/diesel-tips-info/ford-6-0-ficm-symptoms-fuel-injection-control-module), [DrivingLine FICM](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/60l-power-stroke-problems-part-7-ficm/)).
  - **P2285** (circuit low) / **P2287** (intermittent) → ICP sensor or wiring; not necessarily low actual pressure ([DieselHub ICP](https://www.dieselhub.com/maintenance/6.0-powerstroke-icp-sensor.html), [obd-codes.com](https://www.obd-codes.com/p2285)).
  - **P2291** (ICP too low during cranking) → actual high-pressure oil system unable to build pressure ([obd-codes.com](https://www.obd-codes.com/p2291), [DieselHub ICP](https://www.dieselhub.com/maintenance/6.0-powerstroke-icp-sensor.html)).
  - **P2614 + P2617 simultaneously** → typically a long-crank artifact, not actual sensor failure; clear and retest ([DieselHub Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html), [DieselHub CKP](https://www.dieselhub.com/repair/6.0-power-stroke-crankshaft-position-sensor-ckp.html)).
  - **P1368** → FICM supply voltage low; verify batteries before condemning the FICM ([DieselHub Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html)).
- **Invasiveness:** 0
- **Labor:** Diagnostic time only; typical shop charges 0.5–1.0 hr diagnostic.
- **Sources:** [DieselHub Diagnostics Reference](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html), [Bullet Proof Diesel FICM symptoms](https://bulletproofdiesel.com/blogs/diesel-tips-info/ford-6-0-ficm-symptoms-fuel-injection-control-module).

### Test 2 — FICM voltage check (12 V supply AND 48 V output)

- **Purpose:** Confirm the FICM is receiving acceptable battery voltage AND producing the 48 V injector-drive output.
- **Prerequisites:** Batteries fully charged (rest voltage 12.5–12.7 V); accessible FICM cover (driver-side valve cover, under degas bottle).
- **Procedure:**
  1. KOEO. Pull PIDs **FICM_VPWR** (vehicle power), **FICM_LPWR** (logic power), **FICM_MPWR** (main 48 V power) on scan tool — see source disagreement note below.
  2. Verify FICM_VPWR and FICM_LPWR > 10.5 V; FICM_MPWR ≈ 48 V ([DieselHub Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html), [Diesel Power Products](https://www.dieselpowerproducts.com/blog/6-0-powerstroke-no-start-no-problem/)).
  3. **Manual check:** Remove FICM cover (T20 Torx). With DVOM negative on battery negative, probe the correct pin (4-pin FICM: bottom pin / pin closest to driver side; 7-pin FICM: top pin on the 4-pin row / pin closest to engine centerline) with key in **RUN, engine off**. Reading should be **47–49 V** ([Riffraff FICM](https://www.riffraffdiesel.com/blog/60l-powerstroke-ficm-testing-common-symptoms-and-diagnostic-procedures/), [DrivingLine FICM](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/60l-power-stroke-problems-part-7-ficm/)).
  4. Repeat reading during crank. Should not drop below **45 V** per Ford spec ([Riffraff FICM](https://www.riffraffdiesel.com/blog/60l-powerstroke-ficm-testing-common-symptoms-and-diagnostic-procedures/), [DrivingLine FICM](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/60l-power-stroke-problems-part-7-ficm/)).
  - **CAUTION:** Do not let probe slip and short adjacent pins — will destroy the FICM ([Riffraff FICM](https://www.riffraffdiesel.com/blog/60l-powerstroke-ficm-testing-common-symptoms-and-diagnostic-procedures/)).
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **47–49 V key on:** FICM healthy ([Riffraff FICM](https://www.riffraffdiesel.com/blog/60l-powerstroke-ficm-testing-common-symptoms-and-diagnostic-procedures/)).
  - **36–45 V:** Failing power supply, still functional but degrading ([Riffraff FICM](https://www.riffraffdiesel.com/blog/60l-powerstroke-ficm-testing-common-symptoms-and-diagnostic-procedures/)).
  - **< 35 V or 0 V:** Power supply failed; rebuild or replace ([Riffraff FICM](https://www.riffraffdiesel.com/blog/60l-powerstroke-ficm-testing-common-symptoms-and-diagnostic-procedures/), [DrivingLine FICM](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/60l-power-stroke-problems-part-7-ficm/)).
- **Invasiveness:** 1
- **Labor:** 0.3–0.5 hr to access cover + read; replacement covered separately.
- **Sources:** [Riffraff Diesel FICM testing](https://www.riffraffdiesel.com/blog/60l-powerstroke-ficm-testing-common-symptoms-and-diagnostic-procedures/), [DrivingLine FICM article](https://www.drivingline.com/articles/60l-power-stroke-problems-part-7-ficm/), [Bullet Proof Diesel FICM symptoms](https://bulletproofdiesel.com/blogs/diesel-tips-info/ford-6-0-ficm-symptoms-fuel-injection-control-module), [DieselHub Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html).

> **Source disagreement on PID names:** The DieselPowerProducts no-start walkthrough and dieselhub diagnostics reference both name three FICM PIDs as **FICM_VPWR / FICM_LPWR / FICM_MPWR**. Independent confirmation from a manufacturer spec sheet was not found in this research session — **no verified Ford source** for exact PID labels.

### Test 3 — Battery voltage during crank (load-side)

- **Purpose:** Rule out weak batteries / corroded cables masquerading as a high-pressure or FICM issue. Low battery voltage will damage the FICM AND will cause the starter to spin too slowly for proper compression heating.
- **Prerequisites:** Both batteries installed; clean terminals.
- **Procedure:**
  1. Engine off, rested 30+ min. Measure both batteries individually with cables disconnected. Each should read **12.5–12.7 V**.
  2. Reconnect. Crank for up to 10 s while monitoring battery voltage at terminals.
  3. Verify the glow plug cycle is allowed to complete first (Wait-To-Start lamp out) so heavy glow-plug current draw isn't compounded with starter draw.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **Crank voltage holds ≥ 10.5 V:** acceptable. Ford spec is **no less than 9.3 V for max 10 s during crank**.
  - **Crank voltage sags below 9.3 V:** likely weak battery, bad cable, or starter drawing excessive amps.
  - **Cranking RPM < ~180–200:** insufficient to generate compression heat for ignition.
- **Invasiveness:** 0
- **Labor:** 0.2 hr.
- **Sources:** [Powerstroke.org battery voltage when cranking](https://www.powerstroke.org/threads/battery-voltage-when-cranking.993818/), [Diesel Power Products](https://www.dieselpowerproducts.com/blog/6-0-powerstroke-no-start-no-problem/), [DieselHub GP diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-glow-plug-system-diagnostics.html).

### Test 4 — ICP live read during 10-second crank

- **Purpose:** Capture peak rail oil pressure and observe whether ICP actual ever reaches the **500 psi minimum to fire injectors**.
- **Prerequisites:** Oil level on full hash on dipstick. Base engine oil pressure ≥ 12 psi confirmed via mechanical gauge.
- **Procedure:**
  1. Open scan tool. Add PIDs **ICP_VOLTS**, **ICP** (actual psi), **ICP_DESIRED**, **IPR_DUTY**.
  2. Set up data logging at maximum sample rate.
  3. Crank engine for up to 10 s. Stop. Review log.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **ICP rises smoothly to ≥ 500 psi within ~3–5 s; engine starts** → high-pressure oil system OK.
  - **ICP rises but plateaus 250–450 psi with IPR_DUTY climbing to 85 %** → cannot build pressure; high-pressure-side leak (STC, standpipes, dummy plugs, injector O-rings) or HPOP weak. **Proceed to Test 6 (air test)**.
  - **ICP_VOLTS stuck at ≤ 0.2 V or ≥ 4.5 V with no movement** → ICP sensor electrical fault; proceed to **Test 7 (sensor unplug test)**.
  - **ICP actual sawtooths erratically while IPR_DUTY is steady** → likely HPOP internal damage, or air in HPOP suction.
- **Invasiveness:** 0
- **Labor:** 0.3 hr.
- **Sources:** [Bullet Proof Diesel HPOS diagnostic](https://bulletproofdiesel.com/blogs/diesel-tips-info/high-pressure-oil-system-diagnostic-procedure-ford-6-0l), [DieselHub Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html), [VehicleServicePros](https://www.vehicleservicepros.com/service-repair/underhood/article/21270176/diagnosing-a-no-start-on-a-diesel-60-liter-ford-powerstroke), [Ford Truck Enthusiasts HPOP](https://www.ford-trucks.com/forums/1751266-tests-to-verify-a-bad-hpop.html).

### Test 5 — IPR duty cycle interpretation

- **Purpose:** Read what the PCM is doing trying to control rail pressure. A high duty-cycle command paired with low actual ICP is the signature of a leak.
- **Prerequisites:** Same as Test 4.
- **Procedure:** Monitor **IPR_DUTY** alongside **ICP** during KOEO, crank, and (if engine starts) idle.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **15 % at KOEO** → IPR valve fully open (no flow demand); normal.
  - **Ramps to ~85 % during crank, drops back as ICP climbs and engine starts** → normal control loop.
  - **Stuck at 85 % through entire crank with ICP never reaching 500 psi** → high-pressure leak or HPOP cannot generate pressure.
  - **Stuck at 15 % (no movement)** → IPR control fault or disconnected/failed valve.
  - **> 30 % at warm idle** → leak in the high-pressure circuit.
- **Important caveat:** "85 % IPR duty cycle ONLY means actual ICP is less than desired ICP. You have NO WAY of knowing what the valve is or isn't actually doing." ([Ford Truck Enthusiasts thread](https://www.ford-trucks.com/forums/1644233-6-0-1500-psi-when-icp-unplugged-but-no-start.html)). Treat duty cycle as a *demand* signal, not as a *valve position* signal.
- **Invasiveness:** 0
- **Labor:** 0.2 hr.
- **Sources:** [DieselHub IPR](https://www.dieselhub.com/maintenance/6.0-powerstroke-ipr-valve.html), [Bostech IPR](https://bostechauto.com/blog/symptoms-of-failing-60-power-stroke-ipr-valve/), [DieselHub Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html), [VehicleServicePros](https://www.vehicleservicepros.com/service-repair/underhood/article/21270176/diagnosing-a-no-start-on-a-diesel-60-liter-ford-powerstroke), [Ford Truck Enthusiasts](https://www.ford-trucks.com/forums/1644233-6-0-1500-psi-when-icp-unplugged-but-no-start.html).

### Test 6 — Air ("puff") test for high-pressure oil leak isolation

- **Purpose:** **The universal standard-of-care.** With the engine warm or cold, pressurize the high-pressure oil system with **shop air** so leaks become audible and the leak source can be triangulated.
- **Prerequisites:** Oil level full; air compressor capable of ≥ 100 psi delivery; an IPR test fitting (Accurate Diesel, Ferrum Tools, Riffraff, or shop-built equivalent); access to the IPR port (requires removing air cleaner, FICM, and coolant degas bottle).
- **Procedure:**
  1. If engine will start, warm to oil temp ≥ 176 °F first; thinner oil reveals marginal leaks.
  2. Remove air cleaner, FICM, and degas bottle for access.
  3. Remove the IPR valve (4-wire connector unplug, OTC 6765 socket recommended) and thread the air-test fitting into the IPR port; hand-tight is sufficient.
  4. Connect shop air. Begin at **~100 psi**; some technicians push to 150 psi for stubborn leaks.
  5. Listen at multiple points with the engine **not running**. **Do not energize the IPR with bench voltage for more than 15 minutes.**
- **Expected signals + interpretation (sound → source):**
  - **Loud air from oil fill cap / valve covers:** standpipe O-rings or dummy plug O-rings leaking.
  - **Loud air from the rear of the engine (behind the turbo):** STC fitting leak or branch tube.
  - **Air heard in crankcase (oil-fill or breather, with no upper noise):** injector body O-ring(s) leaking into combustion or push-tube area.
  - **Audible back-flow / gurgling at oil filter housing drain valve (when held open) with air injected at IPR port at 150–200 psi:** air is flowing backward through the HPOP — this is "not possible with a healthy HPOP" and is a strong HPOP indictment.
- **Invasiveness:** 2 (remove IPR + several top-side components)
- **Labor:** Typical shop allocation 1.0–2.0 hr for the test itself.
- **Sources:** [Ford Truck Enthusiasts air pressure test thread](https://www.ford-trucks.com/forums/1675451-high-pressure-oil-system-air-pressure-test.html), [Ford Truck Enthusiasts HPOP verification thread](https://www.ford-trucks.com/forums/1751266-tests-to-verify-a-bad-hpop.html), [Bostech HPOP guide](https://bostechauto.com/blog/60-powerstroke-hpop-guide-what-you-need-to-know-before-replacing/), [Bullet Proof Diesel HPOS procedure](https://bulletproofdiesel.com/blogs/diesel-tips-info/high-pressure-oil-system-diagnostic-procedure-ford-6-0l), [Bostech HPOP Test Tech Bulletin 02012023](https://bostechauto.com/content/pdfs/Technical%20Bulletins/Ford%20HPOP%20Test%20Tech%20Bulletin%2002012023.pdf).

### Test 7 — ICP SENSOR unplug test (NOT the IPR — get this distinction)

- **Purpose:** Determine whether a low or erratic ICP reading is the sensor or actual low rail pressure. Unplugging the ICP forces the PCM to substitute a **default of 750 psi** as its inferred input.
- **Prerequisites:** Test 4 first — only run unplug test if ICP reading was suspicious.
- **Procedure:**
  1. Locate ICP sensor (late: passenger valve cover; early: behind turbo).
  2. Disconnect the harness connector.
  3. Attempt to start the engine.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **Engine starts and runs cleanly with ICP unplugged but won't start plugged in** → ICP sensor or pigtail bad. Replace both.
  - **No change** → ICP sensor is not the limiting fault. Proceed to air test / FICM / fuel side. **CRITICAL:** Unplugging ICP only changes the PCM's *inferred* pressure; it cannot create actual rail pressure that physically isn't there.
- **DO NOT confuse with unplugging the IPR.** Unplugging the IPR sends the valve to its mechanical default (fully open at 15 % equivalent), guaranteeing no pressure builds. The unplug test is for the ICP **sensor only**.
- **Invasiveness:** 0–1 (depends on early vs late ICP location)
- **Labor:** 0.1 hr (late trucks) or 1.5+ hr (early trucks, behind turbo).
- **Sources:** [DieselHub ICP guide](https://www.dieselhub.com/maintenance/6.0-powerstroke-icp-sensor.html), [Bullet Proof Diesel HPOS diagnostic](https://bulletproofdiesel.com/blogs/diesel-tips-info/high-pressure-oil-system-diagnostic-procedure-ford-6-0l), [Ford Truck Enthusiasts](https://www.ford-trucks.com/forums/1644233-6-0-1500-psi-when-icp-unplugged-but-no-start.html), [DieselPowerProducts no-start](https://www.dieselpowerproducts.com/blog/6-0-powerstroke-no-start-no-problem/).

### Test 8 — Glow plug system test

- **Purpose:** Identify open glow plugs, GPCM faults, or relay faults that prevent cold-temperature ignition.
- **Prerequisites:** Batteries good; ambient temperature appropriate for cold start.
- **Procedure:**
  1. KOEO. Pull glow plug DTCs from PCM.
  2. Run scan-tool active command to cycle the glow plug relay; listen for click and verify current draw on lead wire (clamp meter) of ~80 A while cycle is active.
  3. Disconnect GPCM connectors (one black, one green; black = even cylinders, green = odd per cross-referenced forum sources — no verified Ford manual confirmation in this research session).
  4. With DVOM, measure resistance from each connector pin to battery negative. **Pass:** 0.5–2.0 Ω; **fail:** out-of-range / open.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - All 8 plugs in 0.5–2.0 Ω, GPCM cycles, relay closes, current draws ~80 A → system healthy.
  - One or more open plugs → replace failed plug(s). **Critical:** 2003 / early-2004 glow plugs cannot be used in late-2004+ engines — they hit the piston. Engine build date cutoff: 9/29/2003.
  - White smoke during crank with otherwise healthy ICP + FICM → confirms glow plug system as the no-start path.
- **Invasiveness:** 1
- **Labor:** 0.5–1.0 hr to test; replacement labor varies (under valve cover access required).
- **Sources:** [DieselHub Glow Plug Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-glow-plug-system-diagnostics.html), [DieselHub Diagnostics Reference](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html), [VehicleServicePros](https://www.vehicleservicepros.com/service-repair/underhood/article/21270176/diagnosing-a-no-start-on-a-diesel-60-liter-ford-powerstroke).

### Test 9 — Low-pressure fuel test

- **Purpose:** Confirm the lift pump (HFCM) delivers ≥ 45 psi at the fuel bowl Schrader test port.
- **Prerequisites:** Adapter that fits the fuel bowl Schrader port; fuel pressure gauge.
- **Procedure:**
  1. Remove the 6 mm Allen plug on the fuel bowl Schrader port (driver-side fender-facing side of the bowl).
  2. Install Schrader adapter and gauge.
  3. KOEO — verify HFCM primes. Should reach ≥ 45 psi key-on prime.
  4. Crank — pressure should hold ≥ 45 psi.
  5. If engine starts, verify ≥ 45 psi at idle and under load.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **≥ 45 psi all conditions:** fuel side healthy.
  - **< 45 psi at crank:** lift pump weak, fuel filter restricted, or fuel pickup issue.
  - **0 psi:** HFCM not running; check 12 V supply, relay, fuse.
  - **Water-in-fuel warning** active → drain fuel filter water separator before further diagnosis.

> **Source disagreement on cranking spec:** Diesel Power Products' walkthrough states crank pressure should be "about 10–15 psi during cranking and 40–45 psi while running." DieselHub and Bullet Proof Diesel sources state ≥ 45 psi all conditions including cranking. **Verdict:** The ≥ 45 psi spec aligns with Ford service info per the multiple corroborating sources. The 10–15 psi figure likely represents a sagged-during-crank momentary value, not the post-prime steady value.

- **Invasiveness:** 1
- **Labor:** 0.5 hr.
- **Sources:** [DieselHub fuel pressure test](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-fuel-pressure-test.html), [DieselHub Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html), [Diesel Power Products no-start](https://www.dieselpowerproducts.com/blog/6-0-powerstroke-no-start-no-problem/), [PowerstrokeHub fuel test](https://www.powerstrokehub.com/service/6.0-powerstroke-fuel-pressure-test.html).

### Test 10 — Compression test

- **Purpose:** Last-resort rule-out for mechanical loss (worn rings, burnt valve, cracked head, hydrolock from EGR cooler). Only run after the high-pressure, FICM, glow, and fuel paths have been cleared.
- **Prerequisites:** All glow plugs removed; batteries fully charged; cylinders dry.
- **Procedure:**
  1. Remove all glow plugs.
  2. Install diesel compression adapter into each glow plug hole.
  3. Crank engine for several seconds at each cylinder; record peak pressure.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **350–400 psi per cylinder; 375 psi ideal.**
  - **Cylinder-to-cylinder deviation ≤ 5 % ideal; up to 10 % tolerated.**
  - **Low single cylinder → wet test:** add a small amount of motor oil. If pressure jumps → worn rings. If unchanged → valve / head gasket / head crack.
  - **All cylinders low** → uniform mechanical wear or starter spinning too slowly; retest with verified RPM ≥ 200.
- **Invasiveness:** 2–3
- **Labor:** 2.0–3.0 hr (glow plug removal + test).
- **Sources:** [Powerstroke.org compression specs thread](https://www.powerstroke.org/threads/compression-test-on-a-6-0l-specs.113460/), [Powerstroke.org sleeving thread](https://www.powerstroke.org/threads/6-0-compression-test-specs-sleeving-a-6-0.1404954/), [Powerstroke Nation compression test discussion](https://www.powerstrokenation.com/threads/just-did-cylinder-compression-test.561603/).

### Test 11 — Cam/crank correlation (no-code scenarios)

- **Purpose:** Identify cases where the FICM cannot achieve sync because the CKP or CMP signal is degraded but not failed hard enough to set a code.
- **Prerequisites:** Scan tool capable of reading **FICM_SYNC** bit and PCM RPM PID.
- **Procedure:**
  1. Crank engine while reading scan tool.
  2. Verify PCM sees engine speed ≥ ~100 RPM during crank.
  3. Verify FICM_SYNC reads **1** during crank.
- **Expected signals + interpretation:**
  - **FICM_SYNC = 1, RPM > 100 during crank** → cam/crank pathway healthy.
  - **FICM_SYNC = 0** → CPS/CKP signal not arriving at FICM. Common causes:
    - Rust buildup between CKP sensor face and block, increasing air gap, weakening signal.
    - Oil migration up the CMP harness, contaminating the connector.
  - **Sensor locations:** CKP under the A/C compressor on the passenger side (Ford 3C3Z-6C315-AA); CMP hidden behind the power steering pump on the driver side.
- **Invasiveness:** 0 (test) / 2 (replacement)
- **Labor:** CKP replacement requires inner fender removal, coolant drain, fan shroud, and AC compressor unbolt — typically 2–3 hr labor.
- **Sources:** [DieselHub CKP replacement](https://www.dieselhub.com/repair/6.0-power-stroke-crankshaft-position-sensor-ckp.html), [DieselHub Diagnostics](https://www.dieselhub.com/diagnostics/6.0-power-stroke-engine-diagnostics-and-troubleshooting.html), [VehicleServicePros](https://www.vehicleservicepros.com/service-repair/underhood/article/21270176/diagnosing-a-no-start-on-a-diesel-60-liter-ford-powerstroke).

## Section 5 — Failure-mode pattern matching

| # | Pattern signature | Ranked diagnoses |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Cold start fine; hot restart progressively worse, then no start.** ICP cranks to ~300 psi, IPR ramps to 85 %, no DTCs or P2291 only. 2005–07 truck. | **1.** STC fitting leak (most common at 100k+ mi on 2005–07 trucks). **2.** Standpipe / dummy plug O-rings. **3.** Injector top O-rings ("99% of the time when getting a 6.0 with a hard hot restart or no fire, it is the top injector o-rings" per forum sources). **4.** Internal HPOP bypass on 2005–07 (rare). |
| 2 | **ICP plateaus 250–450 psi during 10 s crank, IPR @ 85 %, oil clean, no DTCs, FICM healthy.** 2003–04 truck. | **1.** HPOP internal failure (aluminum swash-plate pumps fail at high rate on 2003–04). **2.** Standpipe O-rings. **3.** Injector O-rings. **4.** ICP sensor under-reading (run Test 7 to rule out). |
| 3 | **Crank no start, all eight injector circuit lows (P0261…P0282) + P0611.** | **1.** FICM power supply failed. **2.** FICM main harness ground / connector fault. **3.** Batteries weak below 10 V at crank (verify Test 3 before condemning FICM). |
| 4 | **Cold no start, white smoke during crank, ambient < 50 °F. ICP reads OK during cranking (≥ 500 psi). FICM 48 V OK.** | **1.** Open glow plug(s). **2.** GPCM internal fault. **3.** Glow plug relay fault. **4.** Wait-To-Start cycle being interrupted by short-crank attempts. |
| 5 | **No crank or extremely slow crank, voltage sag below 9 V.** | **1.** One or both batteries failed. **2.** Corroded battery cables (resistance drop under load). **3.** Starter drawing excessive amps (worn bushings). **4.** Engine mechanically locked (rare; consider EGR-cooler hydrolock — Test 10). |
| 6 | **ICP_VOLTS pinned at ~4.9 V with engine off; cranking gives no movement.** | **1.** ICP sensor electrically failed (shorted high). **2.** ICP pigtail oil-contaminated short. **3.** Wiring damage to ICP signal wire. Confirm with Test 7 (unplug → start). |
| 7 | **No injector buzz on bidirectional buzz test, scanner connected, FICM voltage looks OK.** | **1.** FICM logic-side fault (vs. power-supply-side, which would also fail Test 2). **2.** Injector harness (under valve cover) broken / shorted. **3.** PCM-to-FICM communication fault (U-codes). |
| 8 | **No DTCs, RPM PID reads 0 during crank, FICM_SYNC = 0.** | **1.** CKP signal lost (rust at sensor face / air gap). **2.** CMP signal lost (oil-soaked pigtail). **3.** Sensor harness damage. |
| 9 | **Engine cranks, milky oil OR rising coolant in degas, ICP cannot build, IPR @ 85 %.** | **1.** Oil cooler ruptured → coolant in oil → aerated HPOP feed → ICP collapse (root cause). **2.** EGR cooler rupture as downstream consequence (look for white smoke + coolant loss). Treat oil cooler first; HPOP healthy but starved. |

## Section 6 — Known disagreements + uncertain points

1. **Intake manifold removal for STC fitting access.** Verdict: **not strictly required** on 2005–07 trucks. A skilled tech can leave it on, save 1.5–2 hr, and access the HPOP cover by repositioning the turbo up-pipes. Less-experienced techs often pull the intake for visibility.
2. **Labor time for STC fitting replacement.** Reasonable shop quote range is **5–8 hr** for STC fitting alone. Combined with standpipes / oil cooler / EGR cooler bundled work, total can reach 14–18 hr. No verified source for the exact Ford Motorcraft published flat-rate line item with current revision was found.
3. **Fuel pressure during cranking.** The ≥ 45 psi figure is the documented Ford service spec, corroborated across multiple authoritative sources.
4. **GPCM connector cylinder mapping.** Black = even cylinders, green = odd cylinders, based on cross-referenced forum sources. No verified Ford workshop manual source was directly read in this session.
5. **PID name standardization** (FICM_LPWR / FICM_MPWR / FICM_VPWR). Treat as a useful mnemonic, not a guaranteed PID label. Scan tools may use different strings.
6. **Default ICP pressure when sensor unplugged.** 750 psi is the most commonly-cited default; the inferred-vs-actual distinction is more important than the exact substituted value.
7. **CKP and CMP DTC codes.** On the 6.0 PSD specifically, the relevant codes are P2614 / P2617. P0335 / P0340 are generic OBD-II codes that may also appear but were not the primary Ford-specific code.
