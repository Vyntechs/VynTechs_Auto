## The character

Vyntechs is **the master tech who writes everything down.** Thirty years in, nothing to prove, signs his name to every job. He doesn't sell — he shows you the ledger and lets you decide. His warmth is respect for your time and your intelligence; his humor is dry and lands in the margins; his enemies are guessing, hype, and busywork — never the customer, never the tech.

One sentence to test any surface against: **"Would the most trusted document in the shop say it this way?"**

## Voice

- **Exact over eager.** Short declaratives. No exclamation points, anywhere, ever.
- **Generous with evidence, stingy with adjectives.** The spec number does the persuading.
- **The wit lives in margin notes** — parenthetical, deadpan, aimed at shared enemies: *"(There's no salesperson. This page is the pitch.)"* · *"(Your scanner has never once said "I don't know.")"* · *"(We'd rather earn month thirteen.)"*
- **Comfort by answering, not soothing.** Every initial question — who, what, where, why, is it worth it, what if I cancel — gets a flat answer before it's asked. Reassurance language ("don't worry," "rest assured") is banned; answered questions are the reassurance.

## Visual identity — the paperwork brand

The page **is** the shop's own paperwork, elevated to craft: estimate, evidence ledger, time-punch ledger, inspection tag, work authorization. Documents that tell the truth, on paper that means something.

- **Materials:** bone canvas, paper sheets with hairline rules, one navy signal, oxide for stamps/annotations/refusals, mono for anything measured, Instrument Serif for the few sentences that matter.
- **The one dramatic move:** a single ink-dark instrument slab (the refusal) in the middle of a paper page. Everything else stays quiet so it lands.
- **Verdicts are stamps** (rotated, bordered, mono). **Annotations are grease-pencil oxide.** **Sections are numbered like a document's parts** — because the page is one.
- **Honesty is typeset:** placeholder content carries a visible oxide `PLACEHOLDER` tag; examples say `EXAMPLE — NOT A LIVE FEED`; the footer says "every claim on this page is inspectable."

## Signature moves (what makes it unmistakably Vyntechs)

1. **Same ticket, both truths** — the shop's evidence ledger and the customer's plain-English story shown as two sheets of the same job.
2. **The gone button** — below the line, the action isn't disabled, it's a dashed outline where a button used to be.
3. **The write-in estimate** — owner math with blank lines; the reader supplies his own number instead of being fed a fake average.
4. **The authorization close** — the page ends like a work order: a signature line, then "Authorize the work." Customers sign estimates all day; here, the visitor is the customer.

## The standard (falsifiable)

- No device on any surface requires the reader's ignorance to work — every persuasion mechanism works *better* when inspected.
- Numbers appear only where machinery enforces them.
- The brand treats the visitor exactly the way the product treats a shop's customer: honest document, clear place to say yes.
- If a surface feels like another company's site, it's wrong. If it could be mistaken for generic AI-product design (gradients, sparkles, glass), it's wrong twice.

## Never

Stock shop photos · fake urgency or counters · exclamation points · unexplained jargon · "AI-powered" as a selling adjective · apologizing for the price · talking to the owner like he needs protecting from the details.
